OK so I'm planning to narrow down and ship a v1 version, these are the core features:
Core Game Loop

Player is a father who immigrated to a new country with a young child, faked their way into a translator job at a local office to earn money and provide for the family
Three locations: home, office, convenience store
Player earns money at the office by doing translation work, spends money at the convenience store to buy food and items, brings them home to care for the child
Most art assets already exist for these three locations

Child System (not fully finalized, still need to think and debate about some things)

Child has two visible bars: Hunger bar and Happiness bar
Hunger bar depletes daily — requires regular feeding to maintain
Happiness bar depletes more slowly — requires fulfilling wants and requests
Child has visual states reflecting bar levels — energetic and happy when both full, sad and tired when both low
Child dialogue changes based on current state — says different things when hungry, happy, sad, or neglected
Child grows through age stages over time — toddler, young child, older child — each stage unlocks new needs, new dialogue, new items to buy
Push notifications tied to child state — "Your child is hungry" and "Your child misses you" instead of generic practice reminders
Child reacts emotionally when receiving food or items — visible happy reaction that serves as the emotional payoff of the whole loop

Translation Job (Core Learning Mechanic)

Player goes to the office where clients arrive and request translations
Client asks player to translate words, phrases, or sentences into the target language — e.g. "How do you say I would like a coffee please in French?"
Player can answer by speaking out loud, typing, or multiple choice tapping
Correct answer earns +$0.30, wrong answer penalizes -$0.10
Client variety — different client types represent different vocabulary domains: teenager (casual/entertainment), doctor (medical), barista (food/service), traveler (transport/directions), office worker (professional/business)
Client request board at the start of each office session — player sees available clients for the day and chooses which ones to serve, allowing domain-specific practice if desired
Occasional surprise gift from client at end of their session — a food item or small object that can be brought home to the child
Post-session review — player can review which words and phrases they got right or wrong
Translator level system — leveling up increases earning rate per correct answer but also introduces harder clients and more complex phrases
Domain reputation system — serving specific client types repeatedly builds reputation in that domain, unlocking better paying clients in that specialty over time

Daily Quest System

Limited number of story quests per day — typically 3-4
Story quests tied to child's needs and wants — "Child wants to eat hamburger", "Child wants mini toy tank", "Child wants new sofa", "Unlock new room for child"
Non-story achievement quests alongside story quests — "Get 10 translations correct in a row", "Help 10 clients today", "Reach translator level 3"
Quest completion gives instant coin rewards
Daily quest limit prevents players from finishing all content too quickly and paces the game naturally
After daily quests are complete players can continue practicing at the office freely for additional earnings

Convenience Store

Player visits store to purchase items earned through translation work
Food items — hamburger, sandwich, rice, snacks, drinks — each satisfying the child's hunger by different amounts
Toy and gift items — fulfilling the child's happiness requests
Furniture and room items — purchased to upgrade the home
Item variety expands as the child grows through age stages — toddler items replaced by child items replaced by older child items over time

Home And Upgrade System

Home is the emotional center of the game — where the child lives and where care happens
Furniture items have multiple upgrade levels — sofa has 4 levels, bed has 4 levels, etc.
New rooms can be unlocked and built — expanding the home as the child grows
Visual improvement of the home over time reflects the family's improving situation
Child's happiness bar receives ongoing boost from well-furnished and upgraded rooms

Here's rough game story you can tell to people:
Imagine you’re playing as a father who has just immigrated to a new country with his young child. You don’t speak the local language very well, you have almost no money left, but you still need to make a living and provide for your kid. Somehow, you’ve managed to bluff your way into a translator job at a local office, even though your vocabulary is still very limited. Now, every day, you have to show up and actually do the job.

At the office, clients come in one by one: a teenager who wants to know how to say song lyrics, a doctor who needs medical terms translated, a barista who wants to learn how to take coffee orders, a traveler asking for directions, and so on. Your job is to translate what they need into the target language you’re learning, whether that’s French, Japanese, Spanish, or any other language.

You can answer by speaking out loud, typing, or choosing from multiple-choice options depending on your preference. Every correct answer earns you money—for example, $0.30 per correct translation—while every wrong answer costs you $0.10. Every mistake has a real impact on your wallet.

Some clients are especially generous and leave you small gifts after their session, such as a bag of tea, a snack, or a small item that you can bring home to your child. You can also choose which clients to serve each day from a request board in the office. If you want to focus on travel vocabulary, you might choose a backpacker or a flight attendant. If you want to learn medical vocabulary, you might choose a nurse or a pharmacist. This keeps the learning directly tied to what you actually want to improve.

As you continue working and improving, your translator level increases. Higher levels mean you earn more money per correct answer, but clients also become more demanding and difficult. There’s always a sense that your career is progressing.

After your shift ends, you take the money you’ve earned and stop by a convenience store to buy whatever your child needs that day. Maybe they want a hamburger, a toy tank, or a new pair of shoes for school. You bring everything home.

When you get home, your child reacts to whatever you’ve brought. They get excited about the new toy, happily eat the food, or proudly show you a drawing they made. It feels like you’re genuinely taking care of a real child rather than simply managing a few game statistics.

Your home can also be upgraded. The sofa has multiple upgrade levels, you can unlock new rooms, and buy better furniture. As the house improves, it genuinely feels like you’re building a home from nothing.

Every day there are a few story-driven quests tied to your child’s needs and wishes, such as:

- “Your child wants a hamburger”
- “Your child wants a new toy”
- “Unlock a new playroom”

There are also side challenges such as:

- Get 10 translations correct in a row
- Help 10 clients today
- Reach Translator Level 3

Completing these challenges gives you instant coin rewards.

The daily quest limit prevents players from grinding through everything in a single session. You complete a certain amount each day and then come back tomorrow. This keeps the experience feeling fresh rather than something that can be rushed and finished.

If you don’t log in for a day, the app sends notifications like:

“Your child is hungry.”

“Your child is feeling sad.”

This feels completely different from a generic notification like “Don’t forget to practice today.” You actually feel a little guilty and want to come back and take care of them.

The most beautiful part is that your child grows up over time.

At first, they’re just a toddler who needs simple food and stuffed animals. Then they become a young child with their own preferences. Later they start school, need supplies, and make friends. Eventually they want a pet, and now you have to take care of both the pet and your family. Finally, they become a teenager who wants a guitar, new sneakers, and starts asking deeper questions about life and why you moved to this country in the first place.
